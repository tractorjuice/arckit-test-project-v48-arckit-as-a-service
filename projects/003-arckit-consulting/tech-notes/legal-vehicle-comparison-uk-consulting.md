## Summary

Comparison of the four primary UK legal vehicle options for a consulting practice with public-benefit commitments: Limited Company (Ltd), Limited Liability Partnership (LLP), Community Interest Company (CIC), and B-Corp certification (which is an overlay on Ltd or LLP). For ArcKit Consulting specifically, the recommended pathway is **Ltd v1, B-Corp certification year 2, Employee Ownership Trust (EOT) consideration year 3-5**. CIC is **not recommended** because the asset lock is structurally incompatible with the cross-subsidy mechanism described in BR-004 / Principle 1.

## Key Findings

### Limited Company (Ltd by shares)

**Profile**:

- Standard UK company limited by shares.
- Regulated under Companies Act 2006.
- Owners are shareholders; directors run the company.
- Profits distributable as dividends or retained.

**Tax**:

- Corporation tax (current main rate 25% for profits over £250,000; 19% small profits rate up to £50,000; marginal relief between).
- VAT registration mandatory above £90,000 turnover.

**Pros**:

- Most flexible; no asset lock, no governance restrictions.
- Standard public-sector procurement vehicle.
- Cross-subsidy as voluntary commitment (no structural barrier).

**Cons**:

- No structural commitment to public-benefit; relies on shareholder discipline.
- External credibility for social mission depends on transparency reporting + B-Corp certification overlay.

### Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)

**Profile**:

- Partnership with limited liability.
- Members ("partners") own the LLP; profits flow through to partners.
- No corporation tax at LLP level; tax applied at partner level.

**Pros**:

- Tax transparency (single layer of tax at partner level).
- Familiar to professional services audiences (law firms, accountancy).

**Cons**:

- Single-founder practice cannot incorporate as LLP in v1 (typically 2+ members required).
- Less flexible for capital-raising / external investment.
- Public sector buyers less familiar with LLPs.
- Cross-subsidy mechanism less standard.

### Community Interest Company (CIC)

**Profile**:

- Form of social enterprise introduced under Part 2 of the Companies (Audit, Investigations and Community Enterprise) Act 2004.
- Asset lock: assets and profits used for the benefit of the community.
- Dividend cap: typically 35% of distributable profits (capped).
- Annual community interest report.
- 37,081 CICs in existence in the UK (2024-2025 financial year, +12% on previous year).

**Pros**:

- Externally-verifiable structural commitment to community benefit.
- Asset lock prevents mission drift.
- Strong external credibility for public-benefit positioning.

**Cons**:

- **Asset lock structurally incompatible with the BR-004 cross-subsidy mechanism** as designed (asset transfer to a separate company that is not itself a CIC or charity is restricted).
- Dividend cap restricts shareholder return.
- Annual community interest report = additional reporting overhead.
- Conversion CIC → Ltd is materially harder than the reverse.

**Why not recommended for ArcKit Consulting**: The cross-subsidy from the consulting practice (Ltd) to ArcKit-as-a-Service (separate Ltd entity for the SaaS product) cannot easily be structured under a CIC asset lock unless both entities are CICs — which would in turn restrict the SaaS product's commercial flexibility (large-enterprise tier pricing, eventual exit options).

### B-Corp Certification (Overlay on Ltd or LLP)

**Profile**:

- Third-party certification by B Lab.
- Requires score of 80+ on the B Impact Assessment (covering governance, workers, community, environment, customers).
- Legal requirement: amend articles of association to commit to consideration of stakeholder interests.
- External verification by B Lab; re-certification every 3 years.
- Annual fee £500–£10,000 depending on revenue.
- UK B-Corp adoption growing (Investing for Good is a B-Corp, Kin and Carta plc was first LSE-listed B-Corp).

**Pros**:

- Externally-verifiable commitment across multiple dimensions.
- Compatible with both Ltd and LLP — no legal restructuring.
- Buyer recognition is growing.
- Network effect (B-Corp community).

**Cons**:

- Certification process 6–18 months — not realistic for v1 incorporation.
- Annual fee.
- B-Corp's score is broad — does not specifically validate SME-affordability commitment.
- Re-certification every 3 years.

### Employee Ownership Trust (EOT) Consideration

**Profile**:

- Trust structure introduced by Finance Act 2014.
- An EOT acquires majority of company shares for and on behalf of employees.
- Tax-advantaged: tax-free for selling owners on certain conditions; income tax exemption up to £3,600 / employee / year on bonuses.
- dxw transitioned to EOT in 2021 as a year-13 governance evolution.

**When to consider**:

- Year 3-5+ when the practice has reached steady-state.
- Founder-transition risk becomes operationally relevant.
- Aligns long-term incentives with employees.

## Recommended Pathway for ArcKit Consulting

| Year | Vehicle / Status | Rationale |
|------|------------------|-----------|
| Y0 (incorporation) | **Ltd** | Maximum flexibility for cross-subsidy mechanism; standard public-sector procurement vehicle |
| Y1 | Ltd + transparency reporting | First annual transparency report demonstrates commitments |
| Y2 | Ltd + **B-Corp certification** | External verification of social / environmental / governance commitments |
| Y3-5 | Ltd + B-Corp + **EOT consideration** | Optional governance evolution; aligns with dxw values comparator |

CIC is explicitly **not adopted** at any stage due to the asset-lock incompatibility with the cross-subsidy mechanism.
